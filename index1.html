What is Cybersecurity Awareness?

Cybersecurity awareness involves being mindful of cybersecurity in day-to-day situations. Recognizing the risks associated with web browsing, email usage, and online interactions forms the core of cybersecurity awareness. As leaders in business, it becomes our duty to instill the understanding that cybersecurity is an integral part of everyone's role.

While not everyone within an organization needs an in-depth grasp of technical concepts such as SPF records and DNS cache poisoning, providing all employees with information relevant to their roles empowers them to navigate the online landscape securelyâ€”both in the workplace and at home. Implementing role-based training, tailored for both technical and non-technical staff, emerges as the most effective strategy to equip individuals with the knowledge required to combat specific cybersecurity threats.

The interpretation of cybersecurity awareness may differ between the general workforce and technical teams. Matters concerning data management, permissions, and regulations are critical for the IT team but may not be directly applicable to other departments. Tailoring training programs to address the unique needs of each team is essential for establishing a cybersecurity awareness initiative that drives sustainable behavioral changes.



Why is Cybersecurity Awareness Important?

Similar to safety incidents, cybersecurity incidents carry a substantial financial burden. If allocating budget for cybersecurity training, tools, or talent poses a challenge, it's essential to view it through the lens of risk management. With the increasing frequency of cyberattacks each year, the risk of neglecting employee education on cybersecurity awareness only escalates.

In the ever-evolving landscape of cyber threats, criminals continually devise new strategies to bypass the latest defensive tools and technologies, infiltrating the inboxes and browsers of unsuspecting employees. In 2021, a staggering 85% of data breaches involved a human element, and 94% of malware found its way through email.

Email attacks, often involving phishing, are pervasive. Phishing is a deceitful tactic where emails mimic legitimate sources to coerce victims into divulging sensitive information such as passwords and credit card numbers. While email spam filters catch many phishing attempts, some still manage to reach your inbox.

Phishing is not only a straightforward attack but is also readily accessible, merely a Google search away. Anyone with access to the dark web can procure a phishing kit as easily as ordering a book from Amazon. As your employees inevitably encounter cyber incidents, preparedness to respond appropriately by reporting threats to your IT or security team is crucial. Fortunately, cybersecurity awareness training serves as an effective defense against phishing attacks.

Effectively defending against phishing and social engineering attacks hinges on understanding the nature of these threats. While they can manifest in various forms, the most prevalent cyber attacks often involve phishing emails requesting usernames, passwords, and personally identifiable information (PII). A prudent approach is to approach any email requesting personal information, especially from an unexpected sender, with healthy skepticism.

Implementing cybersecurity awareness training might seem daunting, particularly for smaller businesses. However, the opportunity cost of neglecting employee training is too steep to overlook. According to IBM, the average cost of a data breach last year amounted to $4.24 million. A significant 38% of companies experienced a loss of business due to a breach, constituting over half of the total financial losses.

By educating your workforce to recognize and thwart these attacks, you can substantially mitigate the risk of a security incident or breach. This proactive approach could be the decisive factor between a costly ransomware infection and a vigilant message to your IT department stating, "This email appears suspicious, so I refrained from opening it."



How To Promote Cyber Security Awareness In Your Organization

1. Crafting an Effective Security Strategy

Embedding security into the organizational culture is crucial to ensuring that every employee comprehends the significance of cybersecurity and the profound impact a data breach can have. Educating employees on best practices, such as creating robust passwords, contributes to fostering a security-oriented mindset. Human error stands as the primary cause of cyber-attacks, and cybercriminals exploit this vulnerability. Developing a comprehensive information security strategy is essential to safeguard sensitive data, minimize threats, and uphold the organization's reputation.

2. Enhancing Defensive Practices

Security policies are only effective when organizations have a thorough and ongoing mechanism for monitoring cybersecurity compliance. Given the dynamic nature of the security landscape, continuous employee training is vital to enable them to respond adeptly to evolving security threats. Regular risk assessments help identify potential vulnerabilities, allowing the implementation of measures to mitigate them effectively.

3. Prioritizing Security Awareness Training

Implementing an effective security awareness program is paramount in training staff to identify and appropriately respond to the evolving array of cybersecurity threats. All employees, irrespective of their organizational level, should undergo this training to equip them with the skills necessary for threat recognition. Security awareness training should be engaging and informative, ensuring that staff grasp the importance of their role in safeguarding the organization's sensitive data.

4. Benefits of Security Awareness Training:

  • Educating staff on cyber threats
  • Heightening awareness of the sensitivity of data on systems
  • Ensuring adherence to procedures
  • Providing guidance on avoiding phishing emails and other scam tactics
  • Reducing the incidence of data breaches
  • Cultivating a culture of heightened security compliance

Top 10 Web Application Security Risks

The Top 10 Web Application Security Risks for 2021 showcase several changes, including three new categories, four with modified names and scopes, and consolidation.
  • A01:2021-Broken Access Control: Has climbed from fifth place, with 94% of applications undergoing testing for broken access control. The 34 Common Weakness Enumerations (CWEs) associated with Broken Access Control exhibited more instances in applications than any other category.

  • A02:2021-Cryptographic Failures: Now holds the second position, formerly known as Sensitive Data Exposure. This shift reflects a renewed emphasis on cryptography-related failures leading to sensitive data exposure or system compromise.

  • A03:2021-Injection: Has moved to the third position, with 94% of applications tested for injection. The 33 CWEs mapped to this category have the second-highest occurrences in applications, now including Cross-site Scripting.

  • A04:2021-Insecure Design: A new addition, focuses on risks associated with design flaws, urging the industry to embrace threat modeling, secure design patterns, principles, and reference architectures for a proactive approach.

  • A05:2021-Security Misconfiguration: Has risen from sixth place, with 90% of applications tested for misconfiguration. The category now encompasses the former XML External Entities (XXE) category.

  • A06:2021-Vulnerable and Outdated Components: Previously titled Using Components with Known Vulnerabilities, has moved up from ninth place. It stands out as the sole category without Common Vulnerability and Exposures (CVEs) mapped to included CWEs, incorporating default exploit and impact weights of 5.0.

  • A07:2021-Identification and Authentication Failures: Previously Broken Authentication, has shifted down from the second position. It now includes CWEs more related to identification failures, remaining an integral part of the Top 10.

  • A08:2021-Software and Data Integrity Failures: A new category, targets assumptions related to software updates, critical data, and CI/CD pipelines without verifying integrity. Insecure Deserialization from 2017 is now part of this broader category.

  • A09:2021-Security Logging and Monitoring Failures: Formerly Insufficient Logging & Monitoring, has moved up from the tenth position. This expanded category encompasses various failures challenging to test but with direct impacts on visibility, incident alerting, and forensics.

  • A10:2021-Server-Side Request Forgery: Is added from the Top 10 community survey, holding the first position. Despite a relatively low incidence rate, it boasts above-average testing coverage and ratings for Exploit and Impact potential. This inclusion underscores the security community's emphasis on its importance, even if not strongly supported by current data.
